## 1. Lesson Title

**What Is Credit and Why It Matters**

## 2. Objective

By the end of this lesson, the beneficiary will be able to:
- Explain what credit is in one sentence
- Name three things that affect a credit score
- Identify one action that helps credit and one that hurts it
- Understand why credit matters for their future

## 3. Plain-Language Teaching

**What is credit?**

Credit is a trust system. When a bank, landlord, or company agrees to work with you based on your reputation for paying people back — that's credit in action.

Think of it like this: If you borrow $20 from a friend and pay it back on Friday, they'll trust you again. If you don't pay it back, they won't. Credit is the grown-up version of that same idea — except companies keep a permanent record of whether you paid on time.

**Your credit score is a number (usually 300-850) that tells companies: "Does this person pay their bills?"**

**Three things that affect your credit score:**

1. **Paying on time** — This is the biggest factor. Pay your bills when they're due. Every late payment stays on your credit report for 7 years.
2. **How much you owe** — Using too much of your available credit (maxing out credit cards) hurts your score.
3. **How long you've had credit** — Older accounts help you. This is why you don't close old credit cards.

**Key terms:**
- **Credit report:** A detailed record of your borrowing and payment history
- **Credit score:** A number calculated from your credit report
- **Credit bureau:** A company that collects and sells credit information (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
- **Trade line:** An account listed on your credit report (credit card, loan, etc.)

## 4. Real-Life Example

**Scenario:** Two cousins, both 18 years old.

**Cousin A** gets a credit card, buys $200 worth of stuff each month, and pays the full balance on time every month. After one year, Cousin A has a credit score around 700.

**Cousin B** gets a credit card, buys $1,800 worth of stuff (maxing out the card), and makes late payments twice. After one year, Cousin B has a credit score around 550.

**The difference?** Cousin A can rent an apartment, get a car loan at a low rate, and might even get a job (some employers check credit). Cousin B will pay higher interest, may get denied for apartments, and could lose job opportunities.

Same family. Same age. Different habits. Different outcomes.

---

## 5. Short Activity

**Activity name:** Your Credit Future

**Instructions:**
1. Write down three things you want to do in the next 10 years that might require good credit (examples: buy a car, rent an apartment, start a business, get a phone plan)
2. For each one, write one sentence about how good credit would help
3. For each one, write one sentence about how bad credit would hurt

**Time needed:** 10 minutes

**Materials needed:** Paper and pen, or a notes app

## 7. Family Legacy Connection

In our family, we've seen both sides of this. Some of us learned about credit the hard way — through denied apartments, high interest rates, and collection calls. That's not a badge of shame, but it's also not a path we need to walk twice.

The reason we're building the Consumer Evidence Command Center and organizing our records is so that the next generation doesn't have to start from zero. You get to learn from what came before — and build something stronger.

Credit is a tool. Like any tool, it can build or it can break. The goal is to hand you the tool when you're ready to build.
